1.2 Identify Stakeholders

About this Phase

Within the overall process – from the very first idea of creating a new curriculum until the rollout of an agreed upon curriculum – a plethora of different people will participate and engage within the project. Before commencing your work, it is indispensable to identify all potentially relevant partners.

At first it is a fundamental step to define your actual team, i.e. the inner team who is directly involved in creating a new or updating an existing curriculum. This project team should consist of both experienced researchers and teachers, as well as novel minds, allowing to challenge and question existing structures and contents. Heterogeneous teams will avoid a common shortcoming of organizational slack, resulting in static curricula which will be at foremost subject to only minor changes.

Having decided on whom to include in the “internal” team, it is likewise important to specify all potentially relevant external parties. These may be persons who are rather passive and just interested in the process outcome, or parties who are actively engaged in key decisions during the design process. To create an overview of all stakeholders, it is highly advisable to create a so-called stakeholder map (cf. below). This matrix, which is based on Mendelow’s famous Power-Interest grid (1991), maps all relevant parties according to two dimensions which affect the curriculum design process: Power and Interest. Power refers to the stakeholders’ impact on the process, e.g. in terms of influencing or even rejecting decisions made by the internal team. The second dimension refers to the interest of stakeholders in the project. Both dimension result in four types of stakeholders, which have to be managed and communicated with specifically. Stakeholders having a high interest in the project and a lot of influence, i.e. power impacting the design process, should be at the heart of your management efforts. Mendelow suggest a close management. Contrarily stakeholders possessing neither power nor interest in the curriculum design process should be of lesser concern to you and your team, regularly monitoring their attitude towards the project, though, will be a reasonable piece of advice.

Before continuing we advise you to list and map all parties that have interest in your curriculum design process within the embedded stakeholder map.

Additional hint: In case you are new to the design of curricula and the potentially relevant stakeholders involved or interested in the process, the Rough Guide to Curriculum Design by Birmingham City University provides a comprehensive list of exemplary stakeholders. These might give you a first impression of whom to depict in your individual list.
